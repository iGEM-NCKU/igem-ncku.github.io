The Problem

With global aging accelerating, degenerative joint diseases have become one of the leading causes of disability. Total Knee Arthroplasty (TKA) — an artificial knee replacement — is often the last and most effective solution for restoring mobility and quality of life.

However, beneath this surgical success lies a hidden threat:
Periprosthetic Joint Infection (PJI) — an infection that forms around the implant surface after surgery. Although its incidence is only around 0.5–2%, once it occurs, it leads to catastrophic consequences such as repeated surgeries, long-term antibiotic use, chronic pain, or even amputation.

The main culprit is biofilm formation — a structured bacterial community encased in a protective extracellular matrix made of polysaccharides, proteins, and extracellular DNA (eDNA). Once established, biofilms block antibiotics and immune responses, turning a treatable infection into a chronic, relapsing one.
